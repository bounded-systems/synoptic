Colors Are Coordinates

Treat a website's colors as points in a perceptual space and they dedupe, collapse to a handful of hues, and turn accessibility from a checkbox into a construction you can prove.

We pulled every color off a website — 42 of them, straight out of the rendered page — and asked a question that sounds naive: how many colors are there, really?

Not "how many hex values did the designer type." How many distinct decisions. The answer turned out to be far fewer than 42, and chasing the difference led through color science, a 2020 color space called OKLab, a 160-year-old law of perception, and — at the end — a Lean proof that a palette can't fail its own contrast check.

1The eye has a resolution

Start with the fact that makes everything else work: every axis of color has a just-noticeable-difference — a threshold below which two values are, to a human, the same. Nudge a color by less than its JND and no one alive can tell. Above it, they can.

This isn't vague. In OKLab, a perceptual distance of about 0.02 is the edge of visibility. So when a stylesheet carries #16221C and something 0.001 off, those aren't two colors — they're one color written twice. The difference is phantom precision: real in the file, invisible in the world.

That single fact means the space of colors is finite. Perception quantizes it. There is a countable number of distinct colors, and it's smaller than you'd guess — which is why 42 extracted colors were never really 42.

2Three dials, and only one is free

OKLab's cylindrical form, OKLCh, gives a color three coordinates that map onto how you actually see: L — how light (the black-and-white-photo axis). C — how colorful, from gray toward neon. H — which hue, an angle 0–360°.

The surprise is how differently they behave. L is spent on contrast. C is capped by the display's gamut. And H is free — nothing in accessibility or gamut constrains which hue you pick. Hue is the one axis you can spend on identity at no cost.

Here's the sharpest consequence: the hue JND depends on chroma. Perceptual hue distance is roughly C × Δh, so at high chroma a 9° shift is visible; at low chroma you can drift 57° unseen. A near-gray can claim almost any hue and be lying.

Run that on the site's palette and 42 colors carrying 19 hue values collapse to 2 hues that actually resolve — one warm, one cool — plus a stack of neutrals. The palette was two decisions and a lot of lightness.

3The colors that lie about themselves

To check it wasn't a quirk of one site, we ran it on the 148 CSS named colors — the red, forestgreen, rebeccapurple you type without thinking.

They collapse too. 148 names → 49 hues + 23 neutrals, with 31 hues shared by multiple names. green, lime, forestgreen, lightgreen, palegreen, darkgreen, darkseagreen, limegreen, and honeydew are one hue at nine lightnesses. maroon is red at low lightness. The dark-/light-/pale- prefixes are encoding lightness and chroma percentages in English.

And some are outright dishonest. aliceblue, azure, mintcream, ivory, snow carry a hue in their name but their chroma is below the JND. aliceblue is not blue — it's a near-white with an imperceptible tint. (snow and white? 0.012 apart — the same color, twice.)

4A palette is one axiom and some ratios

If a color is a point, a derived color is a move from another point — and there's one honest way to move: one axis at a time, multiplication only. Multiplication, because perception is logarithmic (the 160-year-old Weber–Fechner law): you perceive ratios, not absolute steps. One axis at a time, because coupling axes is what makes derivation confusing — CSS's color-mix() moves all axes with one knob, which is exactly why mixing toward white also desaturates.

So a color family is one chosen hue plus a table of (L%, C%, α%) ratios. The site's 42 colors factor into 16 axioms and 26 derivations — 62% of the palette needs no independent value; it's a proof tree over a handful of roots.

5Accessibility mutes color — and it's geometry

WCAG contrast is a ratio on luminance. White-on-black is the max: 21:1. Any requirement carves the lightness axis into bands separated by a forbidden moat, and the count is logarithmic: floor(1 + log(21)/log(ratio)). At AAA (7:1) you get 2 — a floor and a ceiling, nothing usable between.

Then the part nobody tells you. WCAG constrains only lightness — chroma and hue are free of it. So you'd expect accessible palettes to be vivid. They're not, because the gamut's chroma ceiling is a lens — pinched at the light and dark extremes, fattest in the middle. The most saturated colors live at mid-lightness — exactly the band AAA forbids.

Accessible palettes are muted not by choice but by geometry. That restrained, desaturated look of a careful accessible site isn't taste — it's the shape of the gamut meeting the arithmetic of contrast.

6The whole space is a grid

Once each axis is quantized, you can pin the space to a grid — steps a comfortable multiple of the JND. Multiply it out, drop the degenerate corners, and the entire perceptually-distinct color universe is:

7Construct valid. Don't validate.

The normal way to make an accessible palette: pick colors, then check each pairing against a contrast tool, tweak, re-check. Apply the use, hope it's valid. Flip it. Let the set determine the use. Put surfaces in the dark band and text in the light band, leave the moat empty and wide enough that the lightest surface still clears the bar against the darkest text. Now a color's band is its role, and every surface-on-text pairing is accessible by construction. You never test a pair — you can't produce an invalid one.

For a warm-and-cool AAA palette that's 6 surfaces and 6 texts: 36 pairings, all valid, zero checked. The worst is 7.10:1 — not measured-and-lucky, but guaranteed. And because it's a monotonicity argument, it's provable. In Lean 4:

-- if every surface ≤ Smax and every text ≥ Tmin, and the moat is wide enough,
-- then EVERY surface/text pair clears the bar r. no pair is tested.
theorem whole_set (hmoat : r * (Smax + off) ≤ Tmin + off) :
    ∀ s t, s ≤ Smax → Tmin ≤ t → clears off r s t

One chain: r·(s+off) ≤ r·(Smax+off) ≤ Tmin+off ≤ t+off. The ∀ is "the set determines the use" — pick any surface, any text, it's already valid. The kernel checks it. There is no palette in that shape that fails.

Colors are coordinates

That's the whole idea, and it's small: a color is not a name, it's a point. Name it by its coordinates and the redundancy dissolves — maroon becomes red · V4, aliceblue becomes neutral · V10, and two colors that look the same are the same. Move between points by ratios, one axis at a time. Let contrast carve the lightness axis into bands, and let the bands assign the roles. What's left is a finite, honest, provable palette — one you construct correct instead of validating after the fact.

The eye has a resolution. Work at it, and color gets a lot simpler.
